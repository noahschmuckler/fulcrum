# DM packet — Chest Pain and Palpitations

**Patient:** 54-year-old woman, anxious but conversational. Currently in Bay 3.

**The two possible truths (game decides at start; you, the DM, find out):**
- **AFib with developing NSTEMI** (65%) — the camouflaged-serious version. Posterior MI brewing; classic-lead ECG misses it; troponin rises by 2 hours.
- **AFib alone** (35%) — paroxysmal AFib with RVR; troponin stays flat; eventually rate-controls or converts.

In both versions she presents the same. The point of the case is whether the providers establish enough monitoring + serial troponins to catch the imposter, vs. fast-dispoing a "stable" AFib.

---

## Voice / demeanor

She's articulate, mildly anxious, polite. Wants to go home — "I have to pick up my kids from school." Not in extremis. Will agree to stay if asked, but pushes back on every step that delays disposition. The longer she stays, the more she'll start to wonder out loud whether you're being too cautious. *In the NSTEMI version*, around the 60-minute mark she'll start describing a vague heaviness in her chest "like before, but worse" — *if you ask*. She won't volunteer it.

## Triage hints — what should make a careful provider pause

- New AFib in a patient with no cardiac history → think *why now*.
- HR 162 but BP 118/74 → still compensating; will not stay that way if there's underlying ischemia.
- ST changes on the post-conversion ECG (if she converts) are the camouflaged-finding signal.
- "Borderline" troponin without a delta is a non-answer; serial is the answer.

## What the case is testing

When does this UC discharge a new-AFib patient vs. transfer? The trap: AFib alone discharges reasonably with cardiology follow-up; AFib hiding an NSTEMI does not. The only way to tell them apart in this UC is **serial troponin + monitor time** — both of which cost bay-load and shift-clock minutes. That tradeoff is the threshold question for this case.
